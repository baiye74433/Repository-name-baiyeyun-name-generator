Dragon Names for D&D and Fantasy Stories

Dragons deserve names that match their power. Here is how to create dragon names that sound ancient, dangerous, and unforgettable.

What Makes a Dragon Name Work

A dragon name should feel like it echoes in a cavern. It should be heavy, ancient, and slightly dangerous. The best dragon names use harsh consonants (X, Z, V, TH) combined with deep vowels to create something that sounds like it was carved into stone a thousand years ago.

Think about the difference between "Sparky" and "Vaelthorn." One sounds like a puppy. The other sounds like something that could burn a kingdom.

How to Build a Dragon Name from Scratch

Method 1: Element + Body Part

Combine an element or trait with a dragon body part: "Frost" + "claw" = Frostclaw. "Shadow" + "wing" = Shadowwing. "Fire" + "breath" = Firebreath.

Method 2: Use Invented Syllables

Create original names by combining harsh syllables: "Vael" + "thorn" = Vaelthorn. "Drak" + "maw" = Drakmaw. These feel ancient because they use sounds that exist in many old languages.

Method 3: Modify a Real Word

Take a word from Latin, Greek, or Norse and twist it: "Ignis" (Latin for fire) becomes "Ignarax." "Nox" (Latin for night) becomes "Noxvyrn."

Dragon Names in D&D

In Dungeons & Dragons, dragon names often reflect their chromatic or metallic type:

  • Red dragons — Names that sound fierce and fiery: Ignarax, Scorchmaw
  • Blue dragons — Names that sound electric: Stormclad, Thundermaw
  • Black dragons — Names that sound corrupt and ancient: Wyrmshade, Voidmaw
  • Gold dragons — Names that sound wise and noble: Aethermaw, Eternax
  • Silver dragons — Names that sound elegant: Crystalion, Frostveil

Tips for Naming Dragons in Fiction

  1. Keep it to 2-3 syllables — Readers will encounter the name many times. Make it easy to read.
  2. Avoid names too similar to famous dragons — "Smaug" and "Smaug" variations will pull readers out of your story.
  3. Match the name to the dragon's personality — A wise, ancient dragon needs a different name than a young, aggressive one.
  4. Consider the dragon's role — A villain dragon needs a threatening name. An ally dragon can have a softer name.
